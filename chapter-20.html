chapter twenty

Lamech

The night smells like hot asphalt and bug spray and the sweetness of something left too long in the heat.

Emi stands where the pavement cracks and gives way to grass, at the lot’s edge, in the spill of the one streetlight that still works.

Weight on the right hip, eyes unfocused.

You don’t look for them, she’s learned.

She’s not all the way there tonight.

Snow has thinned to watercolor, enough to put gauze over the edges but not enough to lift her out of her own body, which is the whole point of it, which is the only point, the leaving.

She can feel the grit of the concrete through the soles of her shoes and the mosquitoes finding the soft underside of her wrist.

She is awake inside her skin and she hates it, she hates being this close to the surface of herself, this is the place where the bad things live, right here at the surface.

Footsteps from the far end of the lot. Heel-heavy. Listing.

She doesn’t look up.

Maybe forty.

Flannel, the sleeves shoved up past the elbow, cuffs rolled back careless.

Talking before he reaches her—the price, what he wants—the words loose and oiled in his mouth, a man who has never wondered whether he was allowed to want a thing.

She nods at the right intervals, makes the sounds that mean yes, go on.

His hands move when he talks.

And she sees it.

Right on his left forearm, black ink bled at the edges with the years.

A skull.

Grinning. Hollow where the eyes should be, teeth bared in death’s permanent grin

She has seen that skull before.

Not in this ordinary lot with its moths beating themselves stupid.

She has seen it moving at the edge of her vision while her cheek was crushed into a mattress and her mouth was stopped with someone else’s flesh.

Her body knows him before her mind will say it.

He’s still talking. Price. Room.

She makes the sound that means yes and her hand goes into her bag and closes around the handle of the knife and she doesn’t draw it, lets it warm against her palm.

After the house, she started carrying it.

Die, she thinks.

A thing decided in that house and waiting all this time for its hour, and here is its hour, walking toward her in flannel and not even the grace to remember her face.

Of course, her face never mattered.

They carry nothing, the men who do these things.

They walk out clean, they go home and sleep like the just.

He will carry something now, she will give him something to carry.

She will write in his body for once, and he will go to God marked.

She follows him toward the motel.

· · ·

Two doors down from hers.

She’s passed this door a hundred times.

She’s heard the television through the wall, heard music, the ordinary noises of ordinary lives, the laughing, the arguing.

Inside, the room is the same as hers.

The same brown carpet worn to paths.

The same polyester spread the color of old mustard, the color of dried bile.

The same water stain across the ceiling.

A Bible on the nightstand. Gideon’s gold-edged gift, the same one that sits in every drawer in every room.

You’re going to want to watch this, she thinks at it. This is your kind of story.

He sits on the edge of the bed and looks at her with swimming, unmoored focus.

“I’m into the rough stuff,” he says.

She looks at him a long moment.

“You first,” she says.

· · ·

She tears the sheet herself.

Down the center seam where the weave is weakest, a long clean sound.

She binds his wrists to the headboard, two loops, drawn tighter than comfort, and he’s nodding along, soft-mouthed, eager, he thinks he knows how the service ends.

“Stay,” she says.

And goes into the bathroom.

· · ·

The mirror.

The cold tap on full, water hammering the basin.

She looks at the girl in the glass and the girl looks back.

Mascara tracked down from the corners of the eyes.

Hair pulled loose by the wind off the lake.

She has known this face her whole life, watched it in mirrors since she was small enough to stand on the lip of the tub to reach them.

She cups the cold water and presses it to her face.

The heroin is almost gone now, the last of it draining out of her, and the thing rising up to take its place has no name you could buy on any corner.

It comes up through her warm and white, it steadies her hands until they’re stone-still.

She has never in her life been so awake.

She splashes the water once more, and in the water on the glass her own face wavers and for half a second it isn’t her face, it’s the wallpaper of a childhood bedroom, the flowers on it she used to count to leave her body, one flower two flower three flower.

She goes back out.

· · ·

She climbs onto him slow.

Drags her nails light down the length of his chest, through the shirt, and his pupils widen and his breath goes shallow.

She tilts her head.

“Do you remember me?”

He blinks up at her, tries to bring her face into focus, tries to set it against some memory and fails.

Of course he doesn’t remember.

“You don’t remember what you did to me,” she says, voice tender. “How you held me down, fucked me, beat me when I screamed, left me on the floor to bleed and stepped over me on your way out?”

She watches it move across his face—the slow drunk dawning.

She draws the knife.

“We’re gonna have some fun, yeah?”

· · ·

She opens him low and drags the blade up.

Slow

He starts to scream and the scream fills the room, hits the thin walls, comes back at her, and she is not afraid of any of it.

She’s not afraid of the sound, not the smell, not the dark heat of the blood that comes immediate and astonishing, soaking the mustard spread black, running the lines of her palms, climbing her wrists to the sleeve.

And Lamech said unto his wives: Adah and Zillah, hear my voice. I have slain a man for wounding me, a young man for striking me. If Cain shall be avenged sevenfold, truly Lamech seventy and sevenfold.

She stabs again.

Seventy times seven.

And the sound that comes out of her comes up from the rot that’s been packed into the bottom of her since she was eight years old, it has been down there fermenting ever since, going sour, going black, and now it comes up her throat like vomit.

Every single one.

Her stepfather’s gentle butcher hands.

Every truck, every motel, every room that stank of mildew and industrial cleaner.

All of them, pressed into this one body, on this one altar, under this one knife.

This is the offering she makes for every girl who was ever laid on the stone.

“I was screamin’ just like this,” she says, and her voice breaks down the middle. “Wasn’t I, wasn’t I.”

Sorry, he is saying, has been saying. Sorry — sorry — I’m sorry —

“Didn’t feel sorry back then, huh?”

He screams.

Then less.

The demon wearing the skull goes up out of him like smoke and leaves behind only the dumb disappointing fact of a body.

· · ·

She is on the floor.

She doesn’t remember getting down here.

Both hands in front of her face, palms up.

Blood in every line of the palm.

Blood in the beds of the bitten nails, dried rust already in the creases of the knuckles.

Wash me, and I shall be whiter than snow.

She turns them over, turns them back.

They will not come clean, she knows already.

The room reeks of iron.

She gets up.

She takes the torn blanket from the floor and she wraps him in it, tucking, folding, swaddling.

She takes the rough towel from the bathroom rod and she kneels and presses it to the soaked carpet and scrubs.

The blood will not go.

It thins, it spreads. It sinks down into the fibers.

She scrubs harder and the friction wakes the smell up worse and she’s coughing now, deep and tearing, doubling her over the stain she cannot lift.

She scrubs until her arm gives out.

It’s no use.

There is no use.

You cannot clean it.

The voice of thy brother’s blood crieth unto me from the ground.

She stops.

She kneels there in the reek with the wet towel in her hands and she understands that it is done and cannot be undone, world without end.

· · ·

God is coming for her.

A fact, felt physical in the sternum, the great slow weight of a presence turning, orienting, fixing on her in the dark.

She has been God-haunted her whole life, in the good visions and the bad—the bright warm-winged ones that gather close and sing, and the others, the ones with too many joints folded into too small a space.

She sees them now at the edges of her sight, in the corners the lamp can’t reach.

The cherubim with the flaming sword set to guard the gate but the gate is shut against her now, she is the one being driven out, east of Eden, into the land of Nod, with the mark burning on her where everyone can see.

They unfold in the corners, they have too many eyes and all of the eyes are open and all of the eyes are on her.

The murderous prostitute,

He is coming for the murderous prostitute.

He has come.

She can feel the claws of it closing the distance.

She can feel the whole sky leaning down.

They are coming for her, they will take her, they will drag her down to the place prepared for the likes of her, the unwashed, the unwashable.

She leaves.

She doesn’t close the door all the way behind her. She doesn’t look at the wrapped thing on the bed. She doesn’t think about the morning, about who finds it because she can’t think past now.

Her red fingers print themselves on her own door handle, four marks and a thumb.

And the LORD shall pass over the door, and will not suffer the destroyer to come in.

She goes inside.

· · ·

Her room.

The smell of it closes over her: strawberries and stale cigarettes and the shut-in air.

Her things scattered in the lamplight.

A postcard from the general store she never sent, the lake on the front of it, Greetings From in a cheerful arc.

A dried flower on the sill—Satoru brought it once, held it out stiff-armed, not looking at her, and she’d called him an idiot and put it in a water glass and let it dry standing when the water went, because she couldn’t make herself throw it out.

She sits on the edge of the bed.

Her hands won’t stop now.

She needs to go to the place she goes.

The place that has no smell and no sound and no ledger, where nothing is written down, where the body is finally just a thing she’s wearing and not a book they’ve filled.

She needs him.

She can’t go to him like this, can’t walk the path to the cabin with her arms painted to the elbow and the destroyer at her heels, can’t bring this to his door.

But she can find him the other way.

Her bag. Her hands know the kit by feel without her looking—the spoon, the lighter, the little wax-paper fold with what’s left in it.

She doesn’t measure.

She needs all of it, she needs to not be in this body one more second.

The needle finds the dark track-scarred skin on the crook of her arm, the little ruined garden of old marks..

She pushes the plunger down.

And she prays:

Satoru, Satoru, come get me.

· · ·

Warmth.

From the center of the chest first.

The old cold knot at the sternum, blooms, it floods warm outward into the arms, the throat, the cold places behind the knees, the cold knot at the base of the skull.

The lamp blurs into a halo.

Sound stretches and slows and thickens, world going to honey.

She is sinking.

She is rising.

She cannot tell the difference anymore.

· · ·

Satoru is smiling.

The light where he stands has no source.

It falls from everywhere at once and throws no shadow, the light they promised her in the children’s Bible with the gold-leaf pages.

His arms are wide open.

His face is open, no sharp deflecting grin, nothing held back or angled away, just the boy underneath the boy he shows the world.

Around him: presences.

Brightness folded into the form of wings.

They are singing now, she can almost hear it.

Holy, holy, holy.

She runs.

The way she ran when she was small believing that if you ran fast enough and far enough you’d reach the arms at the end of it.

She hits him and his arms close around her, a half-second late the way they always close, surprised by her the way he was always surprised by her.

She is crying and his hand finds her hair, smooths it.

“It’s gonna be okay,” he says.

She’d know his voice anywhere.

“It’s gonna be okay, Emi. I’ve got you, I’m right here, okay?”

And she believes him.

She believes him completely, the way she hasn’t believed a single thing since she was small enough.

Nothing can reach her in the circle of his arms, no skulls, no destroyer, not God Himself with all His terrible accounting.

She is found.

After a whole life of being taken, she is finally, only, found.

Okay, she thinks, against his chest.

Okay.

Okay.

· · ·

Her body on the bed is going cold.

The lamp throws its yellow circle on the ceiling, steady, indifferent.

The postcard lies on the nightstand, the painted lake serene.

The dried flower stands in its empty glass on the sill, gray and brittle.

The blood on her hands is drying dark to rust.

Her chest rises.

Once.

It does not rise again.

Her small stubborn fist of a muscle, that has beaten her through every single thing the world ever did to her body, makes one last attempt.

And stops.

Nothing changes.

Nothing in the world shifts to mark it, no sound, no stirring of the close warm air.

Outside, a car passes on the road, and its headlights slide across the curtain and are gone, and mean nothing.

The destroyer does not come.

There are no claws, there is no dragging down.

Whatever she was so sure was coming for the murderous prostitute does not come and if it comes, it does as folded wings and a boy with his arms open saying I’ve got you.

She is somewhere warm.

She is in the circle of his arms where nothing reaches, and she is nineteen years old, and for the first time since before she can remember she is not afraid, and she is not waiting for the next thing to be done to her, and she is not for sale.

Life just continues.

And Emi doesn’t.

Selah.
