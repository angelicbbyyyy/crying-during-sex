The morning comes in gold.

There is not a single cloud.

The sky is the blue you’d forgotten existed until you left the city to find it again, and the light lies across the kitchen floor in long warm bars and the whole house smells like coffee and your dog.

The dog is named Pavlov.

You named him as a joke.

He is some indeterminate brown mix from the shelter two towns over, big-pawed, dumb in the loving way dogs are dumb, and right now he is sitting on your foot under the table certain that this is helping, certain that his job is to be exactly here.

“Have you seen my keys?”

Kaito, by the door, patting his pockets helpless like a man who has lost his keys in his own house at least one hundred times.

“Counter,” you say, not looking up.

“Not on the counter.”

“Bowl by the door.”

“Not in the—” A pause. “Okay, they were in the bowl.”

“They’re always there.”

“They are not always in the bowl.”

He comes over and kisses the top of your head, and the dog’s tail thumps the floor in approval.

“You want me to drive?”

“No,” you say. “I’ll drive.”

· · ·

You drive with the windows down.

It is a two-hour drive, and you know the way without the map now, the landscape unwinding like always: the green pressing close, the road lifting and falling, the light coming down through the trees in coins.

The wind comes through the open window and pulls at your hair and Kaito has his arm out his side, his hand surfing the air, and the radio is playing one of those old summer songs.

You sing along, you don’t sing well.

Kaito joins in worse than you do, on purpose, and the dog sticks his head between the front seats to see what the noise is about.

You are happy.

That’s a thing you can say now, plainly, without cringing from it.

You are happy. It took years to be able to hold the word without feeling like you were stealing it from someone.

The happiness is real but it does not erase anything; it just sits beside everything the way the living sit beside the dead.

· · ·

The town is the same.

You think how little it has changed, how a place can hold so still while you become a whole different person inside it.

The one main street.

The general store.

The diner with its sign, the sign that flickered five years ago and flickers now, a couple of letters threatening to go out

You park out front, you leave Kaito and the dog in the shade with the windows down and you go in, and the bell over the door makes the same sound it made the morning you sat in a booth ordering eggs you wouldn’t eat, looking for a boy with white hair.

Darlene is behind the counter.

She looks up at the bell and you watch her place you and her whole face opens.

“Well, look at you,” she says.

“Hi, Darlene.”

She comes around the counter and takes both your hands in both of hers, and she holds you out at arm’s length and looks at you.

“You look well,” she says, and she means it.

“You look so well, honey.”

“I’m okay,” you say. “I’m really okay.”

“Sit down, let me get you a coffee—”

“It’s alright.” You squeeze her hands. “I’m not staying. I just… I wanted to say hi, I was passing through and I wanted to come in and say hi to you.”

She looks at you a moment longer, and something passes behind her eyes.

She knows why you’re here, of course she does, in a town of two hundred people word travels and she has known for five years where you’d be headed if you ever came back.

She just nods, and squeezes your hands one more time, and says, “You come back through, you come see me, you hear?”

“I will.”

“And bring your man and the dog. I saw the dog.”

You laugh, and it surprises you how easy it comes.

“I’ll bring the dog.”

· · ·

You walk into the woods alone.

Kaito understands.

He takes the dog’s leash and tips his head toward the water and says take your time, we’ll be right here, and he means it.

The path is unchanged.

Five years and the path is exactly the same, same roots crossing it.

The woods are full of them.

Nothing so dramatic as ghosts, it’s just the place is soaked in them.

You pass the property line and the old wire fence is still there, sagging and you can almost hear it.

You can almost hear Emi’s laugh coming up the path ahead of you, try not to be too depressin’, yeah.

You can almost hear the flat regular sound of an axe in wood, Satoru splitting logs he didn’t need to split because he needed something to do with his hands.

The cabin is still there, the roof he patched still holding.

You don’t go in, you stand in the clearing and you let it be full of them and then you walk down to the water.

The lake is bright.

The sun is all over it, broken into ten thousand pieces of light and the bees are working the wildflowers along the bank, going flower to flower in their patient ancient routine, and a bird is saying the same three notes over and over somewhere in the trees, and the whole world is so stupidly alive.

It is so beautiful.

That was the worst part, five years ago—that it was beautiful even then, even that morning, flat and bright and saying nothing.

It will never change.

It will be exactly this beautiful and exactly this indifferent long after you are gone too.

You don’t hate it anymore.

You stand there until you’ve had your fill of standing there.

Then you hear Kaito’s voice, distant, gentle, from the tree line.

“Hey, you ready?”

And you are.

“Yeah,” you say. “Let’s go.”

· · ·

The cemetery is small and well-kept and sits on a low green rise at the edge of town where the light gets to it all day.

You bring lilies, white ones.

You went back and forth on it for a long time, standing in the flower section of a grocery store two hours from here, and you landed on lilies.

Innocence. That’s what they mean. You looked it up.

Underneath all of it, underneath the drugs and the money and the bodies and the rot, two children who never got to stop being children, two people the world decided early were things instead of people and who spent everything they had trying and failing to disagree.

The two headstones sit close together.

You picked that.

You picked everything—you were the only one who came, in the end, the only one who claimed them, and so it fell to you, and you put them side by side in the green grass because in life they had found each other, brother and sister, and it seemed wrong to put even a foot of earth between them now.

You picked the words, too.

You sat for weeks and in the end you kept them simple.

Their names, the too-short spans of years, and underneath each one, a single line.

You kneel in the grass, it’s warm from the sun.

Pavlov lies down a respectful distance away with his chin on his paws, somehow knowing.

You split the lilies between the two stones.

“Hi,” you say.

“I’m sorry it took me so long to come see you guys.”

The breeze moves through the grass.

Birds are still going, somewhere, those same three notes.

“Five years. God, I’m sorry. I’ve—” You laugh, tearing, and wipe your face with the back of your hand.

“I’ve always been a bit of a coward, haven’t I? That was always the difference between us, you two ran straight at everything and I just… I stood there and I watched.”

The lilies are very white against the gray stone.

“I wanted to wait until I could come and not just.. cry my ass off, until I had something to tell you that wasn’t just I miss you. And I do, I mean.. I miss you so much it still doesn’t feel real, some days. But I wanted to be able to tell you that it’s good.. that my life—it’s good.”

You tell them you finished school, finally, that you walked across a stage in a gown the same color as these flowers and got the piece of paper and your mom cried.

You tell them about Kaito, how you found your way back to each other.

You tell them about the house and the garden you’re bad at and the dog, the dog named Pavlov, you’d have loved that, Satoru, you’d have rolled your eyes so hard.

You tell them about the ordinary mornings, the gold light on the kitchen floor, the keys in the bowl by the door.

You tell them it’s a good life, the kind of life nobody got to have but you.

And then you’re crying, the way you haven’t let yourself in a long time, and the dog lifts his head, and the sun keeps coming down warm on the back of your neck.

· · ·

This is what you have learned, in five years, the thing you came all this way to say.

You cannot save people who do not believe they are worth saving.

You can love them, with everything you have, you can drive three hours into the night, you can stand at the edge and call their names and it will not be enough, because the thing that’s wrong with them lives in a room you don’t have the key to.

They were both standing in that room their whole lives.

You used to think love was a key but love is more like standing outside the door, saying I’m here, I’m here, but sometimes the person inside still can’t hear you over the sound of their own thoughts.

You have learned that this is not your failure.

It took five years to learn it.

It is not your failure that you couldn’t save them, it was never a thing that was on offer.

You were not too late, there was no on-time, there was only a boy and a girl the world had already decided about long before you got there.

Loving them was not a waste, the love was real.

For a little while, in a cabin by a lake, two people who had never once been treated gently got to be treated gently.

You and the old man Hiro and the lemon cake.

For a little while somebody left coffee on the desk, somebody hugged them, somebody learned their order and their birthday and their favorite stupid flavor of cake.

It was never going to save them but it was important.

That is what you came here to understand, love and the loss are not opposites.

Grief is just love that has nowhere left to go, to a fistful of white flowers and a person on her knees saying I’m sorry, and thank you, and I will carry you, and I’m okay.

· · ·

You stay until you’re done crying.

You read the lines on the stones one more time.

Under Emi’s name: Loved. Even by God, in the end. Loved.

Under Satoru’s: Some birds were never caged

You touch the warm stone with your palm, both of them, one and then the other, the way you’d brush the hair back from a sleeping child.

“Okay,” you say softly. “Okay, I’ll come back. I promise I’ll come back, and it won’t be five years this time, alright?”

You stand, your knees are damp from the grass.

The dog gets up and stretches and shakes himself, and Kaito is there at the edge of the cemetery in the gold afternoon light, hands in his pockets, waiting.

He doesn’t say anything, just opens his arm, and you walk into the side of him and he closes it around you and kisses the top of your head.

“You ready?” he says.

You look back once.

The two stones. The white lilies. The green grass and the gold light.

“Yeah,” you say.

And you mean it, you’re ready.

You walk back to the car with the man and the dog, into the bright ordinary afternoon, into the small good life, and behind you the sun lies down warm across the names of the two people you couldn’t save and loved anyway, would do all of it again anyway.

the light shining down from up above, the way it always does,

on everyone,

the saved and the unsaved alike.
