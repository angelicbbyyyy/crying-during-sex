chapter twenty-one

Part One

The cake was yellow.

Lemon, because she’d said it once— out loud into the air of the cabin while she sat on his floor picking the chipped black polish off her thumbnail.

Nobody ever makes lemon things. It’s always chocolate.

And Satoru, who pretends not to keep things, kept it.

He has filed it somewhere in the locked basement of himself with all the other evidence that he is capable of caring about people, the evidence he spends his whole life trying to deny.

He drove forty minutes to the next town for it.

You found out from the receipt left on the counter—the timestamp, four in the afternoon yesterday, while he’d told you he was just going to get some air.

Happy Birthday in green frosting, slightly crooked, done freehand by a bored girl behind a bakery counter.

Twenty candles in a paper sleeve beside the box, because he’d thought of that too.

He had thought about midnight, about her coming through the door in last night’s makeup, about lighting twenty candles and watching the surprise and shame on her face.

She’d said she’d come by after work.

By midnight she hadn’t.

He sat at the table with the box in front of him and didn’t open it and didn’t speak.

You sat across from him and watched the candles stay in their bag.

You told yourself she lost track of time, maybe client ran late or she fell asleep in her room with the lamp on, and in the morning she would come banging through the door laughing at herself, oh my god, I missed my own birthday, I’m the worst, did you guys really get a cake—

You told yourself this until three in the morning.

Then you stopped telling yourself anything at all.

· · ·

By five you were both still awake, wrung-out, hollow-eyed.

He stood at the window with his back to you, and you watched his shoulders—squared, riding a little too high.

“Let’s go check on her,” you said.

The silence stretched long enough that you thought he hadn’t heard.

“Yeah,” he said finally. “Yeah, okay.”

· · ·

The path through the woods at dawn is not the same as during the daytime.

The light comes in gray and sideways, in long bars between the trunks.

Your feet on the packed dirt.

His feet.

The lake somewhere off to the left, unseen but present.

You found the bird at the property line.

Hiro’s fence ran there—old barbed wire strung between leaning gray posts.

The bird was caught in it.

White, or it had been white; its feathers were matted dark in spreading patches now, slicked down.

One wing was bent, a glint of bone showing through where the skin had torn.

Still alive.

Every few seconds it gathered itself and convulsed into effort, broken wing jerking against the wire—and every single attempt drove the barbs deeper into the meat of it, opened the wounds a little wider, pushed the dark a little further out across the white.

Beneath it, in the dust, the blood came down in drops.

Tap.

Tap.

Tap.

“We should help it,” you said.

Satoru looked at the bird for a long time.

“Can’t save something that’s already dying,” he said.

“It’s not dead yet.”

“It will be.”

The bird tried again, the wire went from pink to red where it crossed the wing.

The tapping in the dust came faster.

“We could cut it free—”

“And then what?” His voice was flat.

“Its wing’s broke, it can’t fly. You cut it loose, it just dies slower on the ground, instead of fast up here”

“So we just leave it?”

He didn’t answer.

You stood there together and watched it fight the thing that held it.

You watched it refuse, with everything it had, to understand that the wire only cut because it kept pulling against it.

Some traps are like that, sometimes the thing that has hold of you is also the only thing holding you up, and there is no version of getting free that isn’t also a falling.

Eventually Satoru reached over and took your hand, his fingers cold.

“Come on,” he said, very quiet. “Let’s go.”

You followed him down the path.

Behind you, in the wire, the bird kept trying.

By evening it would be silent but you didn’t know that yet, and you didn’t go back to check.

· · ·

The motel resolved out of the trees, the VACANCY sign with one letter dead, the cracked lot, the few parked trucks, the sedan with the spidered windshield.

Blood on the door.

A smear across the handle, the unmistakable shape of fingers, pulled sideways.

Drying at the edges, old enough it stopped being red.

You stopped walking.

Satoru didn’t.

He was through the door before your mind had finished assembling the word wait, and then you were following because there was nothing else your body knew how to do.

· · ·

The lamp was on, that was the first thing.

The lamp from the night before was still burning its small useless yellow into the morning.

The smell was the second thing, iron, and underneath it something sickly sweet.

And Emi.

On the bed, on her side, her face turned toward the wall, her black hair fanned across the pillow.

One arm flung out, the hand open, palm up, fingers slightly curled.

Satoru crossed the room.

“Emi.”

He put his hands on her shoulder and turned her toward him and her body had no tension, just weight obeying a hand.

Her face came around into the lamplight.

He looked at her face at the gray that had crept into the lips and something in him simply declined.

It turned the fact away at the door of himself and bolted it shut.

“Hey,” he said, almost amused. “Hey, c’mon.”

He gathered her up against him, got an arm behind her shoulders, lifted her so her head lolled against his collarbone.

“Your hands are freezing,” he said.

He took the open cold hand in both of his and chafed it, briskly, the way you’d warm a child’s hands coming in from the snow.

“Jesus, Emi. How many times, tou go out every night in the cold in nothin’, I keep telling you, you never wear enough, you’re gonna catch your death—”

He stopped on the word.

Then kept going, warmed her hand harder.

“Yeah that’s all it is, you’re just cold. You got that bad circulation, you always had it, corpse hands, I used to say, remember, and you’d get all annoyed—”

“So this is nothing, we get the blood moving, you’re fine. Blood just pools when you’re real still, somebody told me that once, you’re just—you been so tired, you work so hard, you walk that whole road every night, ’course you’re tired, ’course you’re sleeping so deep—”

“Satoru.”

“She’s sleepin’,” he whispered, not looking at you.

He was rubbing her hand, he had moved to the other one.

“She had a long night and she’s just—let her sleep, okay, don’t—you’re gonna wake her up—”

You went around to the other side of the bed.

You knelt and you put two fingers against the side of her throat, just under the jaw, the way a tired instructor in a gymnasium had taught you three years ago on a Tuesday afternoon, here, just under the jaw, you’ll feel it if it’s there, you’ll know.

You felt nothing.

You felt the temperature of the room.

“Satoru, she’s—” Your throat closed. “There’s no pulse, she’s not breathing, she’s—”

“Shut up.” It came out of him, the first crack. “Don’t say that.”

He laid her back down, very carefully, and then he laced his hands together over her chest and pressed, and pressed, and pressed.

His white hair fell into his face, his shoulders worked and under his breath, every few compressions, her name, broken off and started again, broken off and started again.

Emi. Emi. C’mon. Emi.

My Emi.

C’mon, the cake’s getting stale, c’mon, you can’t miss your own—c’mon—

And somewhere far underneath the floorboards of him, down past the lamp-lit room and the cold hands and the rhythm of his pressing palms, something had begun to scream.

It was a scream traveling up toward the surface of him slow as smoke rising through a house, room by room, and it would reach him eventually but not yet.

· · ·

You called for the ambulance from the hallway because you could not hear the dispatcher over the sound of him.

You don’t remember most of what came after.

You remember the sirens arriving and thinking how obscene they were, how loud, in a town this quiet.

You remember the paramedics and their awful compassion, the way they had clearly done this before.

You remember that he wouldn’t let go of her hand and that someone had to ease his fingers open one at a time.

You remember your own hands on his shoulders and his name in your mouth, Satoru, Satoru, Satoru.

You remember that he didn’t fight them.

By the time they took her he had stopped saying she was sleeping.

He had gone somewhere and pulled the door shut and left the lights off, and the boy sitting on the edge of that terrible bed was just the house he used to live in.

· · ·

They make you leave eventually.

There are forms.

There are questions with no answers.

A man with a clipboard and a careful voice asks when you last saw her alive and the words won’t assemble in your mouth and he waits, patient.

By the time it is over the sun has fully committed to the sky and the day is obscenely blue.

The cake is still on the table when you get back to the cabin, the candles still in their bag.

You put the whole box into the trash without opening it because you cannot survive the sight of Happy Birthday in green freehand frosting, and you push it down under the other garbage so you won’t have to see the corner of the box.

Satoru sits on the edge of the “bed” in the cabin and looks at a spot on the floor.

“You should sleep,” you tell him.

You have been awake for somewhere past thirty hours.

You think that if he sleeps, something in him will reset, things are always fractionally less impossible on the other side of sleep.

If you can just both go down into the dark for a few hours, you can come back up and begin the unimaginable work of learning how to live in the world that is now.

“Lie down,” you say. “Please, just lie down with me okay?”

And he does.

He lets you take him by the arm and draw him down onto the mattress.

He lets you pull the blanket over the both of you.

He lets you fit yourself against his back and lay your arm across his chest the way you have a hundred times.

“It wasn’t your fault,” you whisper into the back of his neck. “Satoru, do you hear me? It is not your fault.”

He doesn’t answer.

His breathing is slow and even.

You think, good, he’s going under, he’s sleeping, and you feel your own body finally release its grip on the surface, the exhaustion closing a hand around your ankle and pulling, and the last thing in your mind is something—we’re sleeping, we’re both sleeping, and when we wake up we’ll have survived the worst night of their lives, and that’s a place to start—

You go down.

You don’t feel how long he lies there with his eyes open in the dark, listening to your breath go deep and regular.

You don’t feel the moment he understands that the decision is not something he is going to make but something that was made already, hours ago, in the instant her face came around into the light and he knew, underneath all the warming of her hands, he knew.

You don’t feel him slide his weight out from under your arm one careful inch at a time so he won’t wake you.

You don’t feel him stand in the dark and look down at you.

You don’t feel him reach down and draw the blanket up over your bare shoulder so that you won’t get cold.

You don’t hear the door.

· · ·

The night outside is enormous and it is full of sounds: cicadas grinding, the wind high in the black trees, the small live machinery of a world that has never once stopped, not for the bird in the wire and not for Emi and not for him.

He walks the path toward the lake and he is calm.

The static that has lived behind his eyes since he was a child has finally gone quiet.

Mother death is calling.

A voice that has been waiting in the basement of him since he was seven years old standing at a window watching the driveway.

She’s calling and I’m answering. Coming, mother. I’m coming.

He has been told his whole life that God is love, that not one sparrow falls to the ground without the Father knowing, that God numbers the hairs of your head, that God knit you in secret, that God made you on purpose, fearfully and wonderfully, that you are seen and you are kept and you are never, never alone.

The sparrow is in the wire behind him, dying by inches in the dust.

And the LORD said unto Cain, where is Abel thy brother? And he said, I know not: am I my brother’s keeper?

He had promised, that night she came to him drunk he had held her on the floor of the cabin and he had said I won’t leave, and he had meant it.

He had kept the letter of the promise and it bought her nothing, it saved her nothing, she died anyway, alone.

And he said, what hast thou done? The voice of thy brother’s blood crieth unto me from the ground.

He understands now, with an unbearable clarity, that God is real, and that God is a butcher who strokes the animal’s flank to gentle it before the knife.

God makes girls like Emi—tender, strange, funny, packed full of a dark and brilliant light—for the express purpose of watching the world grind every bit of it down to nothing, and then calls the grinding his will.

God hates whores.

God hated her.

God has always hated Satoru, marked him in the womb with his mother’s blue eyes.

The light shines down from up above. And God’s gonna take the ones you love.

He took Emi and he’ll take this too.

That’s fine.

That is, in fact, the only mercy God has ever extended him—the one door he forgot to lock

You’ll think that He has left you,

Has He left you.

Is He gone.

The lake is where it has always been.

Black at the tree line where the dark of the water meets the dark of the woods.

Flat in the center where the late moon has laid its long road of silver across the surface, the sky bent over its own reflection, indifferent, patient.

He thinks of you.

Asleep in the cabin with his blanket pulled up over your shoulder, your arm even now reaching across into the warm dent where his body was.

You who looked at him in a lecture hall a lifetime ago.

You who left coffee on his desk with a doughnut because he’d mentioned once he skipped breakfast.

You who drove three hours into the middle of nowhere and walked into the dark woods alone because you could not stop loving a boy who could not stop running.

I am poison, everything I touch I rot.

I am rotting you, right now, in your sleep, just by lying next to you.

The only act of love I am capable of is to take the poison out of the house, to leave you the cold side of the bed one last time, so that you can get up, and grieve, and go home, and live.

And by the time that you come home, I’ll have left you all alone.

He is sorry, he doesn’t know who he is saying it to.

I’m sorry. I’m sorry. I’m sorry.

The stones on the shore are smooth and heavy and cold, and they fit his hands just right.

He walks down to where the dirt goes dark.

The water is the cold of every winter even now, even in the dead heat of summer.

It takes his feet.

It doesn’t recoil from him, it doesn’t ask what he’s carrying or why.

It receives him the without preference, without judgment.

The silver road of the moon lies just ahead on the surface and he walks out toward it, the warmth waiting at the far end of it, toward his mother’s arms, come, someone is finally, finally going to say it’s okay, it’s gonna be okay, I’ve got you.

The moon’s road lies unbroken on the water.

And then it doesn’t matter, and then there is no him to whom it could matter, and the lake closes its black hand over the last white of his hair.

It is very quiet.

It is so quiet.

The cicadas don’t stop, nor the birds, nor the wind.

The world doesn’t stop, it has never once stopped, for anyone.
