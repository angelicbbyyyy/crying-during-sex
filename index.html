chapter nineteen

I

The nights are getting shorter.

Summer has started its decline, where you can feel autumn waiting in the wings, the slow turn of the earth toward darkness that no amount of wishing can prevent.

You and Emi sit by the lake, when the cicadas are warming up for their prayer and the mosquitoes are just starting to emerge.

You’ve been quiet for a long time, both of you, just sitting with the weight of everything unsaid, everything that’s happened.

Finally you speak.

“Being here has suffocated me.”

Emi doesn’t respond, just keeps looking at the water, and you continue because now that you’ve started you can’t the truth back in the box where it’s been festering.

“I keep thinking about—when my boyfriend came looking for me, my friend too. They drove three hours and I sent them away.”

You pick at the dock’s splintered wood, pull a piece free, watch it fall into the water.

“Maybe I should’ve left with them, maybe I should’ve said fuck this place and just—gone.”

The confession sits between you, true and terrible.

“Part of me feels like I never belonged here.” Your voice cracks slightly. “Like I’ve been playing pretend and I decided to stay because I thought I could fix something, thought I could—” You stop. “I don’t know what I thought.”

Maybe this is what happens when you hang out with people like Emi and Satoru.

Emi is quiet for a long moment, so quiet you think maybe she’s not going to respond at all, maybe you were never meant to understand each other no matter how hard you tried.

Finally she speaks, and her voice is gentle.

“You’re a sweet little girl. This ain’t a town for sweet little girls, never has been.”

“I think you’re a sweet girl too,” you say, and you mean it, even though you know it’s naive.

Emi doesn’t know what to say to that.

She smiles, small and coy and self-deprecating, that smile that says you really don’t know me at all do you?

You’re really bad at judgin’ people, huh?

How could she possibly ever be a sweet little girl when sweet little girls don’t do what she does, don’t have the things done to them that have been done to her?

Sweet little girls don’t whore themselves out, don’t get gang-raped, don’t shoot heroin in gas station bathrooms.

Sweet little girls get protected, get saved, get to grow up believing the world is fundamentally good.

Then, unexpectedly, you reach for her and pull her into a hug.

She feels small underneath your arms, so small it’s almost shocking, like you’re suddenly aware of exactly how little she is, how much space she doesn’t take up, how a girl this tiny has contained this much suffering.

Frail, that’s the word.

She feels frail like bird bones.

Something delicate that the world has been systematically destroying, and you can’t understand how anyone could want to hurt her.

She smells like strawberry shampoo and it makes you smile against her hair because it’s such a normal smell, such a reminder that underneath everything she’s still just nineteen, still just a kid trying to survive in a world that’s been cruel to her.

“I care about you,” you whisper into her hair, quiet enough that she could pretend not to hear if she wanted to.

“Please be careful out there.. please.”

Your arms tighten slightly, protective, futile, because you can’t protect her, can’t save her, can’t do anything except hold her for this moment and pretend that saying please be careful will somehow shield her from the things that are coming.

“You’re gonna make it out of this town,” you tell her, still holding her.

Emi thinks: You’re so naive.

She thinks it with affection, the kind of tender exasperation you feel for someone who still believes in fairy tales when you’ve long since learned that stories don’t have happy endings.

She lets you hold her even though part of her wants to push you away because this makes everything harder, makes the choice she’s slowly making more complicated, makes the end she’s approaching less simple.

When you finally let go, when you pull back to look at her, there are tears on her cheeks.

Barely visible, could be sweat in this humidity.

You pretend that’s what they are, you let her have the dignity of pretending she’s not crying and that she’s still the tough girl who jokes about death and fucks strangers and doesn’t feel things deeply enough to cry about them.

But you both know.

The tears are real and they mean something and that something is too big to name.

You just sit there, side by side.

Summer keeps dying.

II

Satoru’s thoughts are poison.

The alcohol doesn’t help anymore, it makes thoughts that circle like vultures more insistent, more real, more impossible to ignore.

Blood.

Blood.

His blood. On motel sheets when he was seventeen.

On bathroom floors when he cut himself just to feel something.

On his hands in the space behind his eyes where images play on repeat whether he wants them to or not.

Emi’s blood. In the hospital.

On the floor of that house where ten men decided she was less than human.

In his imagination when he thinks about what they did to her, how much of her bled and how much was taken that can never be given back.

The blood haunts him and reminds him that bodies are just meat, they’re temporary containers that eventually rupture, leak and fail in spectacular and mundane ways.

He recalls the night he tried to die.

The pills counted out in his palm, however many it was—not enough, clearly, because he’s still here.

He wishes he’d done it properly, wishes he’d succeeded.

If he’d just died that night then you wouldn’t be here.

You’d be at school, you’d be with your boyfriend or someone like him, someone normal and stable and capable of being loved without destroying the person who loves them.

You’d have a future.

You’d be happy.

You’d be safe from him, from the contamination that comes from proximity to people like him who turn everything they touch into death.

And Emi.

If he’d died he wouldn’t know Emi, wouldn’t have to watch her destroy herself, wouldn’t have to feel this helpless rage every time she climbs into a truck, wouldn’t have to carry the weight of loving someone he can’t save, wouldn’t have to witness her slow-motion suicide while being unable to do anything except participate in his own.

He’d be dead.

Ten feet underground where he belong, decomposin and feeding worms.

Returning to the earth that made the mistake of birthing him in the first place.

The thoughts spiral, and the alcohol that was supposed to quiet them just turns the whisper of you should be dead into a scream.

All that love he spoke about.

When he told you he loved you, when he let those words fall out of his mouth while he was inside you.

How could he ever?

How could he ever think he was worthy of that word—love—when the only thing he’s ever been good at is destroying himself and taking people down with him?

How could he even entertain the thought of loving you? Pure, naive you.

You who still believe in redemption, in the possibility that love can save people.

How could he look at someone like you and think that what he feels is love instead of what it actually is—need, the parasitic attachment of someone who’s dying and sees you as a host, as a life raft, the thing that might keep him breathing a little longer even though it just delays the inevitable.

He needs you.

Needs you like drowning people need air, like addicts need their fix, like parasites need hosts.

It’s not love, a biological imperative of a dying thing trying to attach itself to something living and steal its warmth.

I hate myself.

I hate myself.

I hate myself.

The only certainty in a world where everything else is negotiable and is subject to change except this one fundamental fact: he is worthless, he is a mistake that should have been corrected years ago and every day he continues existing is just another day of inflicting himself on a world that would be measurably better without him in it.

The thoughts get so loud they become pressure in his chest, understanding that his body is a prison and his mind is a torture chamber.

And then he’s crying.

He doesn’t know when it started, just knows that suddenly his face is wet and his breathing is ragged.

Everything’s flooding out at once: the self-hatred, the shame, the certainty that he’s fundamentally broken, the knowledge that he’s destroying you, the understanding that loving him is the worst decision you’ve ever made and he’s too selfish to tell you to leave, too desperate to let you go.

He’s sobbing on the floor of the cabin, and he sees you—when did you come in? how long have you been there?—kneeling next to him, your face worried, your hands reaching for him, and he wants to tell you to run, to get away before he drags you down further.

But he can’t manage anything except collapsing into you when you pull him close, when you gather him into your arms.

You call out to him—Satoru—and he just cries harder, buries his face in your chest, lets himself be held like a child.

You cradle him and it’s too much like that first time he broke in your arms.

He’s that boy again, small broken thing that wants to be held but knows he doesn’t deserve it, that needs you desperately and hates himself for needing you.

Your hands in his hair, gentle, patient, infinitely tender.

You hold him because you don’t know what else to do, this is all you have to offer.

Keep whispering that it’s okay even though it’s not okay.

Satoru keeps crying.

He keeps hating himself for it.
